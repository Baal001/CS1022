When AI Recommendations Go Wrong

Navigating the ethical dilemma of customer data breaches in AI-driven tourism marketing

1 My Profession

Marketing in the Age of AI

A marketing executive develops campaigns that promote products and services through advertising, branding, and digital media. In Singapore, where enterprise AI adoption reached 53%, among the highest globally, marketers rely on machine learning to personalise advertisements (IBM, 2024). AI recommendation systems have transformed tourism marketing by delivering tailored suggestions (OECD, 2024). However, the personal data they require creates vulnerability to breaches and privacy violations (Yallop et al., 2023). When not managed responsibly, technology designed to enhance customer experience becomes a source of harm.

2 The Ethical Dilemma

AI-Driven Recommendations & Data Breaches

The Scenario

You are a manager in a large tourism company that recently implemented an AI recommendation system. The system analyses customer preferences, travel data, and online reviews to provide personalised recommendations. However, customers who relied on these recommendations have started experiencing security breaches, receiving suspicious emails and bank hacks linked to information they entered. Head office is not interested in the complaints, and you cannot instruct customers not to use the system. What do you do?

Learning Outcomes

By exploring this website, you will be able to identify ethical risks in AI-driven marketing, analyse implications for multiple stakeholders, evaluate responses using computational thinking, and connect decisions to your code of ethics.

3 Ethical Implications

Who Is Affected and How?

Travel data breaches expose financial details and personal itineraries, potentially leading to identity theft (Yallop et al., 2023).

Stakeholder Risk Assessment

Customers

Customers face direct harm as payment details and travel histories are compromised, risking fraud and identity theft. Under the PDPA, customers can expect their data is protected with reasonable security measures (PDPC, 2024).

The Manager (You)

As a marketing manager, you face a conflict between corporate loyalty and ethical duty. Head office dismisses the complaints, yet customers are harmed. Remaining silent makes you complicit, while escalating may jeopardise your position.

The Company

The company risks reputational damage, regulatory penalties, and loss of customer loyalty. Under the PDPA, organisations that fail to protect personal data face significant financial penalties and adverse publicity sanctions (PDPC, 2024).

The Wider Community

Unresolved breaches erode public confidence in AI-driven services across the tourism industry. When one company fails, consumers become wary of similar platforms, slowing ethical AI adoption in the sector (OECD, 2024).

4 Navigating the Dilemma

How to Respond Ethically

Using computational thinking, we can decompose this problem into actionable steps that balance professional responsibility with practical realities.

Computational Thinking Applied: Decomposition breaks the dilemma into parts. Pattern recognition links the AI system to breaches. Algorithm design creates a step-by-step response.

Document and Gather Evidence

Collect records of customer complaints, security incidents, and correspondence with head office. Pattern recognition helps identify whether breaches are linked to the AI system, forming the basis for a credible report.

Escalate Through Proper Channels

Present documented evidence to senior management through a formal report. Frame the issue around legal risk under the PDPA and reputational damage. If head office continues to dismiss the matter, escalate to the compliance or legal department.

Advocate for Immediate Safeguards

Propose concrete measures such as a data protection impact assessment, an independent cybersecurity audit, and stronger encryption protocols, aligned with PDPC guidelines (PDPC, 2024).

Notify Affected Customers Transparently

Inform affected customers about the breach and steps being taken. Transparency rebuilds trust and aligns with the SCAP's principle that communications must be truthful (ASAS, 2008).

Seek External Guidance if Necessary

If internal channels fail, consult the PDPC or industry bodies such as CASE. Whistleblower protections support those who report data protection concerns in good faith.

5 Code of Ethics

The SCAP and Your Professional Duty

The Singapore Code of Advertising Practice (SCAP), administered by the Advertising Standards Authority of Singapore (ASAS), is the primary ethical code for marketing in Singapore. Its foundational principle requires all advertising to be legal, decent, honest, and truthful (ASAS, 2008). While last revised in 2008, its core principles remain directly applicable.

Applying SCAP Principles

The SCAP's honesty requirement means marketing professionals must not deceive consumers about data use. Operating a recommendation system that causes breaches while concealing this violates the code's emphasis on truthfulness. The SCAP also requires substantiation of claims, so a company cannot promote a "personalised AI experience" while failing to secure the data powering it.

Beyond the SCAP, the PDPC's 2024 Advisory Guidelines on AI Recommendation Systems reinforce that organisations must implement security safeguards when processing personal data through AI (PDPC, 2024). Singapore's Model AI Governance Framework further emphasises accountability and transparency (IMDA, 2024). Together, these provide a comprehensive ethical foundation for marketers.
